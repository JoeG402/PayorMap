Methodology & Reliability

Data Quality

Source

The data rendered on this map comes from the U.S. Census Bureau's American Community Survey (ACS) 5-year estimates. Specifically, two subject tables feed the two modes of this visualization:

  • Table S2703 — Private Health Insurance Coverage by Type and Selected Characteristics. Used when Private Insurance is selected.
  • Table S2704 — Public Health Insurance Coverage by Type and Selected Characteristics. Used when Public Insurance is selected.

Both tables were introduced by the Census Bureau in 2015 when the combined S2701 table was split into private/public halves. They break down coverage into employer-based, direct-purchase, Tricare/military, Medicare, Medicaid/means-tested, and VA components, intersected with demographic, social, and economic characteristics such as age band, poverty ratio, and full-time work status.

ASC Mode Data Source

The Medicare-Certified ASCs mode uses the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) list of ambulatory surgical centers enrolled in Medicare. Each facility's 5-digit ZIP was joined to a county FIPS via the HUD-USPS ZIP-COUNTY crosswalk (picking the county with the highest total-address share when a ZIP crosses multiple counties) and then aggregated to a per-county count. The source list is maintained by CMS at cms.gov/medicare/enrollment-renewal/providers-suppliers/ambulatory-surgical-centers.

What is a "5-Year Estimate Subject Table"?

The ACS is a continuous rolling survey sent to roughly 3.5 million U.S. addresses per year. It is published in two primary cadences:

  • 1-year estimates are published only for geographies with populations of 20,000 or more. They reflect a single calendar year but carry wider margins of error.
  • 5-year estimates pool five consecutive years of survey responses (e.g., 2019–2023) and are published for every geography down to census block groups. Because they combine more responses, they are statistically more reliable for small areas like rural counties — but they represent an average over the five-year period rather than a point-in-time snapshot.

Subject tables (the "S" prefix, e.g. S2703) are pre-tabulated summaries organized around a single topic. Unlike detailed tables (B-prefix) which show one breakdown, subject tables combine multiple views and include derived statistics such as percentages, medians, and ratios. They are designed to be human-readable without requiring additional computation.

Reliability & Interpretation

Every ACS estimate is published alongside a margin of error (MOE) at the 90% confidence level. An estimate of 14,838 with an MOE of ±177 means: "we are 90% confident the true population value lies between 14,661 and 15,015." A few things to keep in mind when reading this map:

  • MOEs scale with denominator size. Tiny counties (a few hundred residents) can have MOEs that exceed the estimate itself — meaning the value is too noisy to act on. Losing County, TX with a population of 33 is an extreme example: every reported percentage has very large uncertainty.
  • (X) entries in the source tables indicate that an estimate could not be computed because no sample observations, or too few observations, were available.
  • The 2020 survey year suffered from COVID-19 data-collection disruption. Any 5-year period containing 2020 (for example the 2016–2020 or 2018–2022 vintages) has elevated MOEs and should be interpreted with extra caution. The Census Bureau published this data under a waiver of its normal statistical quality standard.
  • Sampling error is not the only source of error. Nonsampling errors — misreporting, coverage error, nonresponse bias — also affect estimates and are not captured in the MOE.

Why Some Counties Are Blank

A county rendered in the neutral "no data" color on this map indicates that no value from S2703 or S2704 could be joined to its polygon. The most visible example is Connecticut, which this map displays as entirely empty.

  • Connecticut — planning regions replaced counties. Connecticut's eight historical counties (FIPS 09001–09015) ceased functioning as governmental or administrative entities in 1960, and in 2022 the Census Bureau formally adopted the state's nine Council of Governments (COG) / planning regions (FIPS 09110–09190) as county-equivalents at Connecticut's request. Beginning with the 2022 ACS release, S2703 and S2704 publish Connecticut data under the new planning-region FIPS codes only — the old eight-county codes receive nothing. Meanwhile, the county geometry used on this map comes from a TIGER/Line vintage that still draws the eight legacy counties. The result is a FIPS mismatch in both directions: the old county polygons have no data, and the new planning-region codes have no polygons. Connecticut therefore appears blank until the boundary file is updated to the nine planning regions.
  • Suppressed small-sample estimates. When a county has too few sample observations for a particular cross-tabulation, the Census Bureau publishes (X) instead of a number. These cells are treated as missing here and will render blank for the affected statistic.
  • FIPS drift. A handful of counties and county-equivalents have been renumbered or renamed over time (Alaska borough reorganizations, Virginia independent-city boundary changes, Puerto Rico and territorial codes) and occasionally fall out of alignment between the boundary file and the data vintage, producing isolated blanks.

Oath Opportunities Logic (also summarized on the map)

The Oath Opportunities mode is intentionally designed to find counties that are attractive on both absolute scale and employer-based insurance penetration. It does not reward counties that are only large, and it does not reward counties that are only high-rate but tiny. The goal is to surface places with a meaningful number of commercially attractive lives and a healthy private employer-based mix.

  • Input 1 — employer-based covered lives. For each county, the model uses Employer-Based Coverage (Alone/Combo) from ACS table S2703. This is the raw covered population count and captures market size.
  • Input 2 — employer-based coverage rate. The model also uses the percentage version of that same measure relative to the county's total civilian population. This captures concentration / quality of mix.
  • Normalization. Each county's raw count and percentage are separately normalized to a 0–1 range using the observed national minimum and maximum values in the underlying county dataset. This puts the size and rate inputs onto a common scale before blending them.
  • Blend method. The final score is the geometric blend of the normalized count and normalized percentage: score = 100 × sqrt(count_normalized × percent_normalized). Using the geometric mean instead of an arithmetic average is deliberate: it penalizes imbalance. A county must perform reasonably well on both dimensions to earn a high score.
  • What this suppresses. Large counties with huge employer-based populations but middling or weak employer-based share are pulled down, because their rate term drags the product lower. Likewise, very small counties with a great employer-based percentage but very few total covered lives are also pulled down, because their count term stays weak.
  • What rises to the top. Counties score best when they combine a large total civilian population, a large employer-based covered population, and a high employer-based coverage share. In practice, the map highlights counties that have both market depth and commercially attractive payer composition.
  • Hover display. In Oath mode, the headline number shown on hover is the employer-based coverage percentage, while the detail rows show total civilian population, Medicare alone, Medicaid alone, and employer-based coverage alone or in combination percentage. This keeps the qualitative readout grounded in both market size and payer mix.

In plain English: the Oath score is a two-factor screen. A county needs enough people to matter and a strong enough employer-based insurance share to be commercially interesting. Excelling in just one of those areas is not enough.

Using This Map

The choropleth colors reflect county-level point estimates only; no statistical-significance test is built into this visualization. A county shown in dim color may have a genuinely low value, or may have an estimate too unreliable to interpret. For policy, research, or journalistic use, always consult the Census Bureau's published tables directly so that MOEs can be read alongside estimates.
